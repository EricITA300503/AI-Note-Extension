You are already on the right track by targeting thoracic mobility with the foam roller and opening up the chest with the doorway stretch. Slouching (often called postural kyphosis) is a two-part problem: the muscles in the front (pecs) get incredibly tight and pull you forward, while the muscles in your upper back (rhomboids, mid/lower traps) get overstretched, weak, and "turn off."

Since you are already tackling the tightness, the missing link is strengthening and activating the back muscles to pull your shoulders back naturally, along with addressing the forward-head posture that almost always accompanies a slouch.

Here are three targeted exercises to add to your routine:

1. Wall Angels (Scapular Control)

This is an exceptional movement for retraining your brain to activate the upper back and shoulder stabilizers while keeping your spine neutral.
Wall Angels Exercise. Fonte: Healthline

How to do it: Stand with your heels, glutes, upper back, and head flat against a wall. Bring your arms up to a "90-degree goalpost" position, trying to get the back of your hands and elbows to touch the wall. Slowly slide your arms up into a "V" shape and back down, focusing on keeping your back and arms pinned to the wall the entire time.

Volume: 2–3 sets of 10–12 controlled reps.

Note: If your hands or elbows lift off, only go as high as you can maintain contact. Mobility will improve over time.

2. Prone YTWLs (Lower and Mid-Trap Strengthening)

This sequence isolates the muscles responsible for keeping your shoulder blades pulled back and down, counteracting the slouched position.
YTWL Positions. Fonte: ACRO: Physical Therapy, Massage Therapy & Aerial Fitness

How to do it: Lie face down on a mat (you can rest your forehead on a rolled-up towel to keep your neck straight). Move your arms into each letter position, keeping your thumbs pointing up toward the ceiling to force external shoulder rotation:

Y: Raise arms at a 45-degree angle. Squeeze lower traps.

T: Raise arms straight out to the sides. Squeeze shoulder blades together.

W: Bend elbows down toward your ribs to form a W. Squeeze mid-back.

L: Keep elbows bent at 90 degrees and rotate your forearms up toward the ceiling.

Volume: Hold each position for 3–5 seconds, doing 5–8 reps per letter.

3. Chin Tucks (Deep Neck Flexor Activation)

When the upper back slauches, the head naturally drifts forward. Chin tucks strengthen the deep muscles in the front of your neck to pull your head back over your shoulders.

How to do it: Sit or stand tall. Look straight ahead and pull your head straight back, as if you are trying to make a "double chin" or pull your ears back over your shoulders. Do not tilt your head down; keep your gaze horizontal.

Volume: Hold for 3 seconds, release, and repeat for 15 reps. You can easily do these throughout the day while sitting at a desk.

Integrating This Into Your Week

Since these are postural activation drills rather than heavy lifting, you can do them 3 to 4 times a week right alongside your current foam rolling and doorway stretches.

Here are a few highly recommended books on posture correction, biomechanics, and structural balance, ranging from highly practical guidebooks to deeper deep-dives into human movement mechanics:

1. The Highly Practical, Step-by-Step Blueprint

"Overcoming Poor Posture: A Systematic Approach to Refining Your Posture for Health and Performance" by Steven Low and Jarlo Ilano  
Goodreads

Why it’s great: Written by a physical therapist and a gymnast/coach, this book skips the fluff and focuses on actionable mechanics. It breaks down exactly why the upper back slouches (the relationship between the thoracic spine, scapulae, and deep neck flexors) and builds a literal roadmap of progressive exercises and stretches to fix it. It is incredibly practical for someone who likes a systematic, structured program.

2. The Functional Movement & Biomechanics Approach

"Move Your DNA: Restore Your Health Through Natural Movement" by Katy Bowman  
Goodreads

Why it’s great: Bowman is a biomechanist who looks at posture through the lens of movement ecology. Instead of just giving you a list of exercises to do for 10 minutes a day, she explains how modern sedentary environments "shape" our tissues over time (like casting a muscle in a mold). It will completely change how you view your setup, how you carry your torso, and how you sit or stand during the day.

3. The Structural Foundation & Body Awareness Guide

"8 Steps to a Pain-Free Back" by Esther Gokhale  
Tom Pfeiffer Fitness

Why it’s great: This is a classic in the posture world. Gokhale spent years studying primal postures in non-industrialized cultures where slouching and back degeneration are incredibly rare. The book is heavily visual and teaches concepts like "stretch sitting" and "shoulder rolls" to naturally reposition your thoracic spine and chest open without feeling forced or rigid.

4. For Deep Functional Anatomy

"Postural Correction" by Jane Johnson  
Human Kinetics

Why it’s great: If you want to know the exact anatomy of what is happening under the skin, this is a phenomenal guide. It clearly maps out which specific muscles become chronically shortened (like the pectoralis minor and anterior deltoids) and which ones become chronically lengthened and weak (like the rhomboids and mid-traps) during a slouch. It provides explicit stretching and strengthening cues based strictly on anatomical evidence.

If you want a direct program to start next week, grab Overcoming Poor Posture. If you want to understand the underlying mechanics of how your everyday habits are shaping your spine, go with Move Your DNA.
