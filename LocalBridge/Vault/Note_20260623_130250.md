It is great that you're already addressing this. The doorway stretch and foam rolling are two of the absolute best ways to open up your chest and mobilize your thoracic spine (upper back)—so keep doing those.

When we get into that "slouched" position (often called hyperkyphosis or rounded shoulders), two things typically happen simultaneously:

The front is too tight: Your chest and anterior shoulders pull you forward.

The back is too weak: The muscles responsible for holding your shoulder blades back and down (rhomboids, lower/mid traps, and rotator cuff) become elongated and inactive.

To fix it, you need to combine the stretching you're already doing with targeted strengthening to pull your shoulders back into alignment.

Here are four highly effective movements you can add to your routine.

1. The Dynamic Stretch: Wall Angels

This movement builds on your foam rolling by actively forcing your upper back into extension while opening up the shoulders.

How to do it: Stand with your heels, glutes, upper back, and head flat against a wall. Bring your arms up to a "90/90" position (like a football field goal), keeping your elbows and the back of your hands pressing into the wall. Slowly slide your hands upward, trying to keep everything in contact with the wall, then pull your elbows back down into your ribs.

Volume: 2–3 sets of 10–12 controlled reps.

Why it works: If your upper back is stiff, your hands or lower back will want to pull away from the wall. Forcing the contact point forces your thoracic spine to straighten out.

2. The Upper Back Builder: Prone Y-T-W Raises

This is a fantastic bodyweight sequence that hits the exact postural muscles that go dormant when we slouch.

1
The 'Y' Position
Lower Traps

Lie face down on the floor with your legs straight. Bring your arms up overhead at a 45-degree angle (forming a 'Y'). Point your thumbs up toward the ceiling. Keeping your forehead down, lift your arms off the ground by squeezing your shoulder blades together. Hold for 2 seconds, then lower.

2
The 'T' Position
Rhomboids & Mid Traps

Move your arms straight out to the sides so your body forms a 'T'. With thumbs still pointing toward the ceiling, lift your arms up by pinching your shoulder blades tightly together. Hold for 2 seconds, then lower.

3
The 'W' Position
Rear Delts & Rotator Cuff

Bend your elbows and pull them down toward your ribs so your arms form a 'W'. Lift your elbows and hands off the floor simultaneously, focusing on driving the backs of your shoulders toward the ceiling. Hold for 2 seconds, then lower.

Volume: Do 6–8 reps of each position consecutively to complete one full set. Aim for 3 sets.

3. The Deep Stabilizer: Chin Tucks

Slouched posture almost always goes hand-in-hand with a "forward head" posture. This exercise strengthens the deep neck flexors to pull your head back over your spine.

How to do it: Sit or stand tall. Look straight ahead, and look for a point on the wall to keep your eyes on. Without looking down, pull your head straight back, as if you are trying to make a "double chin" or pull your ears back over your shoulders. Hold for 2–3 seconds, then release.

Volume: 2 sets of 15 reps. You can easily do these sitting at a desk.

4. The Functional Pull: Face Pulls

If you have access to a gym cable machine or a resistance band anchored at home, this is the gold standard for fixing rounded shoulders.

How to do it: Anchor a band or cable rope at upper-chest or eye level. Hold the ends with your thumbs pointing backward. Step back to create tension. Pull the center of the rope straight toward your nose, while simultaneously flaring your elbows out and pulling your hands apart. At the end of the movement, your hands should be next to your ears, and your shoulder blades should be completely pinched.

Volume: 3 sets of 15–20 reps with light, controllable resistance. Focus entirely on the squeeze.

A quick cue for daily life: Instead of trying to force your chest out or pulling your shoulders straight back (which can cause you to arch your lower back), focus on pulling your shoulder blades down into your back pockets. This instantly stabilizes the upper back without putting stress on your spine.

If you want to dive deeper into the mechanics of your posture, a few excellent books approach the human body from different angles—ranging from actionable, step-by-step exercise programs to deep dives into biomechanics (how the body moves) and functional movement habits.

Here are four of the best books on the subject, depending on what you want to focus on:

1. For the Practical Gym/Home Routine
"Overcoming Poor Posture" by Steven Low and Jarlo Ilano

The Vibe: Direct, systematic, and highly evidence-based.

What it covers: Written by a physical therapist and a gymnastic strength coach, this book skips the fluff and treats posture as a training issue. It gives a clear, comprehensive breakdown of exactly why certain areas get slouched (like the thoracic spine and forward head) and provides structured, progressive exercise routines to fix them. It’s perfect if you just want an effective, step-by-step programming template to run with.

2. For Re-learning How to Move and Sit Daily
"8 Steps to a Pain-Free Back" by Esther Gokhale

The Vibe: Anthropological and structural.  
Booktopia

What it covers: Gokhale looks at traditional societies around the world where back pain and slouching are practically non-existent. The book focuses heavily on resetting your primal structural alignment. Instead of giving you a gym workout, it teaches you how to sit, stand, walk, and sleep differently. It includes deep visual guides on concepts like "stretch-sitting" and "shoulder repositioning" to naturally keep your chest open without constantly forcing it.  
Booktopia

3. For Mind-Muscle Connection & Structural Awareness
"The New Rules of Posture: How to Sit, Stand, and Move in the Modern World" by Mary Bond

The Vibe: Somatic (mind-body connection) and anatomy-focused.

What it covers: Mary Bond is a master of Structural Integration (a form of bodywork focused on alignment). This book is phenomenal for identifying the mental habits and daily mechanics that keep you slumped over your desk or phone. It uses highly descriptive awareness exercises to help you feel how your bones and deep fascia (connective tissue) sit, teaching you how to stabilize your core and spine effortlessly from the inside out.

4. For the Bigger Biomechanical Picture
"Move Your DNA: Restore Your Health Through Natural Movement" by Katy Bowman

The Vibe: Dynamic, scientific, and lifestyle-oriented.

What it covers: Bowman is a biomechanist who argues that our bodies are literally shaped by our environment and modern movement "sedentarism." She views a slouched back not just as a weak muscle, but as a lack of varied movement "nutrition." This book is incredible for understanding the mechanics of how tiny cellular loads affect your overall structure, offering actionable lifestyle shifts and micro-stretches to realign your entire frame.

A quick recommendation on where to start: If you want immediate, hard exercises to add to a fitness routine, grab Overcoming Poor Posture. If you want to change how you sit at a desk or hold yourself throughout the day, start with 8 Steps to a Pain-Free Back.
