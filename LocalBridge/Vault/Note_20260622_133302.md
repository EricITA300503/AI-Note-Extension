That slouched upper back position—often called "thoracic kyphosis" or just the classic "desk hunch"—is super common, especially if you spend a lot of time coding or working at a screen.

It is awesome that you are already doing the doorway chest stretch and foam rolling. Those are fantastic for mobility (opening things up), but mobility is only half the battle. To actually fix a slouch long-term, you have to pair that openness with strength. You need to wake up the muscles in your upper back (like your rhomboids, lower trapezius, and rear deltoids) so they have the strength to pull your shoulders back and hold you upright automatically.

Here is a balanced, highly effective routine you can add to what you are already doing.

1. The Mobility Upgrades (Keep Loosening Up)

Since you already use a foam roller, let’s make sure you're getting the most out of it, and add one active mobility drill.

Foam Roller Thoracic Extensions: Instead of just rolling up and down, support your neck with your hands, place the roller under your mid-back, and gently extend your upper back over the roller (keeping your butt on the ground). Hold for a few seconds, then move the roller up an inch and repeat. Avoid arching your lower back.

Cat-Cow Stretch: On your hands and knees, alternate between arching your back toward the ceiling (pushing the floor away) and letting your belly drop while lifting your chest. It introduces fluid movement to a stiff spine.

2. The Posture Builders (Start Strengthening)

These exercises target the exact muscles that counteract a slouch. You don't need heavy weights for these; focus entirely on feeling the squeeze between your shoulder blades.

Prone Y-T-W Extensions

Lie face down on the floor with your forehead resting lightly on the ground. You will move your arms into three positions, holding each contraction for 2 seconds. Do 10–12 reps per letter.

The Y: Put your arms out at a 45-degree angle (forming a Y) with your thumbs pointing toward the ceiling. Lift your arms by squeezing your lower shoulder blades together.

The T: Bring your arms straight out to the sides (forming a T), thumbs still pointing up. Lift your arms, squeezing your mid-back.

The W: Bend your elbows and pull them down toward your ribs (forming a W). Lift your hands and elbows off the ground together, pinching your shoulder blades tightly.

Wall Angels

Stand with your heels, butt, upper back, and head flat against a wall. Bring your arms up so your elbows and the backs of your hands touch the wall at 90 degrees (like a cactus).

Slowly slide your hands up the wall into a "Y" position, then slide them back down, tucking your elbows toward your ribs.

The challenge: Keep your lower back, elbows, and hands pressed against the wall the entire time. If your hands lift off, only go as high as you can maintain contact. Do 2 sets of 10 reps.

3. A Quick Progress Checklist
Exercise	Type	Targets	Volume
Foam Roller Extensions	Mobility	Thoracic spine stiffness	5-6 spots, 3 extensions each
Prone Y-T-Ws	Strength	Lower/mid traps & rear delts	1-2 sets of 10-12 reps per letter
Wall Angels	Control/Strength	Mid-back & shoulder rotation	2 sets of 10 slow reps

A quick tip on consistency: Your chest stretch and foam rolling are great to do daily. For the strength exercises (the Y-T-Ws and Wall Angels), try hitting them 3 to 4 times a week. It takes a few weeks for the nervous system to start holding that new baseline posture, but you'll likely feel "taller" right after doing them.

Recommendation on where to start: If you want a quick, highly visual routine to fix a desk-slouch, start with Sit Up Straight. If you want a deeper, exercise-science approach to programming mobility and strength together, grab Overcoming Poor Posture.
